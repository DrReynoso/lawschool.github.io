Issues

Is more than intent to make contact necessary to meet the intent element in a battery claim?

Rule

Intent to make contact, and not necessarily harm, is necessary to meet the intent element of battery claim.

Analysis

No. For contact to amount to battery, it must be intentional and must be harmful or offensive. To meet the intent element, the tortfeasor need not have intended that the contact be harmful or offensive. Rather, intent to make contact is all that is necessary to meet the intent element. An act is defined as an external manifestation of the actors will and does not include any of its result, even the most direct, immediate, and intended. In other words, an actor need not appreciate the results of his intent to make contact for the act to constitute battery. It is enough that the result of harm or offense occurs. In this case, then, it is immaterial for the first element of battery whether Giese could form the intent to harm Wagner. It is enough that Giese formed the intent to make contact with Wagner. When this intent is combined with the resulting harm to Wagner, the elements for battery are met. Because the state is immune from battery claims, the lower courts dismissal of Wagners claim is affirmed.

Whether legal insanity operates as a defense to liability for intentional torts.

Rule

Where a legally insane person causes intentional damage to the person or property of another, he is liable for that damage in the same circumstances in which a normal person would be liable.

Analysis

No. Almy is liable for the assault and battery she intentionally committed against McGuire. Where a legally insane person causes intentional damage to the person or property of another, he is liable for that damage in the same circumstances in which a normal person would be liable. Thus, if a particular intent is required for tort liability for a normal person, the insane person cannot be liable for the same tort unless he is capable of entertaining that same intent and actually entertained it at the time of committing the tort. For a normal person to be liable for assault and battery, he must act with intent to cause apprehension of harm in another person, or intent to cause actual harm to another person. As the only question presented involves an intentional tort, it is unnecessary to decide at this time whether this rule of liability for insane persons also applies to torts resulting from negligence. It is enough to presently find Almy liable for assault and battery because she possessed the requisite intent to cause damage to McGuire. Judgment on the verdict is entered for McGuire.

Under this standard, attorneys are required to act in good faith, using their legal skills and knowledge to represent their clients to the best of their ability. Here, there is no evidence that Topping and Carter violated any duty of care or good faith. They prosecuted Hodgess suits to the best of their knowledge, using a practice for service of process that was customary for North Carolina attorneys and had never been challenged in a court of law. Thus, because Topping and Carter acted in accordance with the practices of numerous other North Carolina attorneys and simply made a mistake of law without knowing any better, they cannot be held to have breached their duty of care owed to Hodges. The judgment of the trial court is affirmed.

Issues

Is the standard of care applied to those in the medical profession a national standard?

Rule

The standard of care applied to those in the medical profession is a national standard, not a local standard.

Analysis

Yes. The standard of care applied to those in the medical profession is a national standard, not a local standard. The rationale for holding doctors to a local standard of care is that doctors in rural areas without access to cutting edge technology or even up-to-date knowledge should not be held to the same standard as doctors in more urban areas with such access. This rationale, however, does not apply here because the lab in this case is located in Washington, D.C., a very urban area with access to the most cutting edge technology and medical knowledge. In addition, the recent rise in transportation and communication availability as well as the standardization of medical practice in general makes the application of a local standard of care even more suspect. The instruction effectively and improperly excluded Morrisons expert witnesss testimony that the national standard for urethral smear tests is that they are conducted with the patient sitting or lying down. As a result, the judgment is vacated and the case is remanded for a new trial.

Court

Court of Appeals of New York

Facts

The decedent of Martin (plaintiff) was killed when a buggy he was driving collided with an automobile driven by Herzog (defendant). A statute required all buggies to be operated with headlights at night. At the time of the accident, Martins decedent was violating this statute by not driving a buggy with headlights. Martin brought suit against Herzog for negligence. Herzog countered by stating that Martins decedent was liable for contributory negligence based on his violation of the headlight statute. At trial, the jury held for Martin and found Herzog liable for negligence. The appellate court reversed, and Martin appealed.

Issues

Whether the failure to perform an act required by statute constitutes negligence per se.

Rule

Where observance of a statue would subject a person to danger which he might avoid by disregard of the general rule, it would be unreasonable to adhere automatic rule of negligence.

Analysis

Yes. Herzog is not liable for damages because Martins decedent engaged in contributory negligence by violating the headlight statute. An omission, or failure to perform an act required by statute, constitutes negligence per se. When a statute requires an affirmative action, the failure to perform that action constitutes a violation of a legal duty. It is negligence per se. The violator may be liable for damages, but only if the omission is the proximate cause of the injury. Thus, with the headlight statute, Martins decedent will only be liable for contributory negligence for failing to use headlights if that omission was the proximate cause of the disaster. If Martins decedent had been using the headlights as required, Herzog likely would have seen his buggy in the night and would have been able to avoid the fatal accident. Thus, the decedents failure to use lights constitutes negligence per se. It does not matter that Martins decedent is not the defendant. Violating a statute may be contributory negligence just as it may be negligence. Herzog is not liable for damages because Martins decedent engaged in contributory negligence by violating the headlight statute. The decision of the appellate court is affirmed.
